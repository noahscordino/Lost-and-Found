Pricing assessment guide

Your field reference for every pickup. Use the calculator to lock in your offer before you speak. Never let the seller set the price — you know the market, they don't.

The golden rule: never pay more than 25% of expected sell price
If it sells for $20, your max offer is $5. This is non-negotiable.

Surf, skate & vintage — flat rate rule
When you can't identify the brand or it's genuinely vintage, use this rule. No calculation needed.
🏄
$5–10 flat per piece
Any surf, skate or vintage item where the brand is unclear, unlabelled, or genuinely old — offer $5 for good condition, $10 for near-new or standout. Do not go higher without running the full calculator first.
Applies to: Billabong, Rip Curl, Quiksilver, Volcom, O'Neill, Rusty, Hurley, Santa Cruz, Independent, Thrasher, unbranded vintage tees, old skate decks with clothing, mystery deadstock, market finds

At 25% max: you pay $5, keep $10. That's a 200% ROI — healthy. At 40% (overpaying): you pay $8, keep $7. One slow-moving item wipes that out. Stay at 25% or below, always.
Non-negotiable rules
Print these. Read them before every pickup run.
✕
Never let the seller name a price
Their opinion of value is irrelevant. You set the offer. If they name a number first, ignore it and give yours.
✕
Never pay for "paid $200 new"
Retail price means nothing in secondhand. What it cost new is irrelevant to what it sells for used.
✕
Never pay for sentiment
"This was my mum's" or "it's special to me" does not change the market rate. Acknowledge it, hold your price.
✕
Never pay cash for C-tier
Kmart, Target, no-label, damaged. Politely offer to donate it. Never pay cash. It will sit on your rack forever.
✕
Never go item-by-item with a seller
Total the bag and make one offer. The moment you go piece-by-piece you invite negotiation on every single item.
✓
It's okay to walk away
You are doing them a favour by collecting. You do not owe them a high price. Walking away is sometimes the right call.
✓
Offer once, hold firm
Make your offer, explain your reasoning briefly, and stop talking. Silence after your offer is not awkward — it's professional.
✓
Condition beats brand every time
A near-new Glassons dress can beat a fair-condition Carhartt on margin. Always assess condition first, brand second.
Field scripts — what to say when pushed back
Have these memorised. Calm, confident, and honest always wins.
Pushback #1
They say: "But I paid $200 for this new!"
"I totally get that — and it's a great piece. But the secondhand market doesn't really reflect what things cost new. I'm going off what I can actually sell it for, and my offer is based on that. That's the honest number for me."
Pushback #2
They say: "Trade Me has one listed for $80."
"Listed for, sure — but sold for is a very different story. A listing doesn't mean anyone's buying at that price. I track what things actually sell for, and that's what I'm basing this on."
Pushback #3
They get offended or upset by the offer.
"I really appreciate you choosing us and I'm not trying to be unfair. This is genuinely what the numbers look like for me to make it work as a business. I'd rather be straight with you than offer more than I can stand behind. I'm happy to take everything else as a donation so nothing goes to waste."
Pushback #4
They want to sell specific items only and keep others.
"Totally fine — I'll go through what you've got and make you an offer on the pieces that work for me. No pressure on anything you want to keep."
Decline
The whole bag is C-tier or poor condition — nothing worth paying for.
"Honestly, looking through this — the pieces here aren't something I can pay for on the resell side. But I'm more than happy to take everything as a donation so it all goes to a good home rather than landfill. That work for you?"
Surf / Skate / Vintage
You find something clearly surf/skate/vintage but you're not sure of the brand or era.
"This one's interesting — I can offer $5 for it in this condition, $10 if it's near-new. It's a flat rate I use for vintage and surf pieces where I'm not 100% sure of the resell yet. Happy to take it at that."
Deploying updates to GitHub Pages
Every time you get an updated HTML file, follow these steps to push it live. Takes about 2 minutes.
1
Go to your GitHub repo
Go to github.com and open your Lost & Found repository. Your main site file should be named index.html in the root folder.
2
Click the file you want to update
Click index.html (or pricing-guide.html) in your file list to open it.
3
Click the pencil icon (Edit)
Top right of the file view — click the pencil ✏ icon to open the editor.
4
Select all and paste the new code
Press Ctrl+A (or Cmd+A on Mac) to select everything, then paste the new HTML from the file Claude gave you. The whole file replaces the old one.
5
Commit the change
Scroll to the bottom. In the "Commit changes" box, add a short note like "Update pricing model", then click Commit changes. GitHub Pages will rebuild automatically — live within 1–2 minutes.
6
Check it's live
Visit your domain (or yourusername.github.io/repo-name) and hard-refresh with Ctrl+Shift+R (Cmd+Shift+R on Mac) to bypass the cache. If it's not showing yet, wait 60 seconds and try again.
Custom domain tip: Add a file called CNAME (no extension) to your repo containing just your domain name on one line — e.g. lost-and-found.co.nz. Then point your domain's DNS A record to GitHub's IPs: 185.199.108.153, 185.199.109.153, 185.199.110.153, 185.199.111.153. Changes can take up to 24 hours to propagate.
